## 1. Title — 1615: The Summer Siege of Osaka

Sixteen fifteen. The Summer Siege of Osaka.

This was the final clash for the realm — the battle that decided who would rule Japan,
and the tactical archetypes that defined it.

What follows visualizes the fourteen major commanders who fought here: their strategic
deployments, and their ultimate fates.

---

## 2. The Board is Set

Two armies. Two fates.

The Tokugawa faction represented the consolidation of power — a massive, overwhelming
force designed to secure the newly established Edo Shogunate. Their ranks were filled
with the Shogun's own bloodline, and elite veteran vanguard units.

The Toyotomi faction were the last heroes, fighting in desperation. The defenders of
Osaka Castle, waging a doomed war of resistance. Their ranks were a volatile mix of
brilliant tacticians, loyalist politicians, and ronin seeking one final, honorable stand.

---

## 3. Opposing Philosophies of Command

Two commanders. Two philosophies of war.

Tokugawa Hidetada — thirty-six years old, third son of Ieyasu, second Shogun of the
Edo Shogunate. He arrived late to the Battle of Sekigahara, but proved a calm, capable
politician. At Osaka, he led the massive Tokugawa army from the rear — directing his
generals, but never risking himself on the front line.

Ono Harunaga stood in stark contrast. A senior retainer and close aide to Toyotomi
Hideyori and Lady Yodo, he commanded from the central headquarters inside Osaka Castle
itself. A moderate who favored peace negotiations, he managed the castle's political
affairs and served as the central coordinator of its overall defense — and in the end,
he committed suicide alongside his lord and lady.

Rear-guard command against front-line desperation. This was the shape of the war.

---

## 4. The Shogun's Bloodline Encirclement

Ieyasu did not send strangers to finish this war. He sent his own blood.

Matsudaira Tadanao, twenty years old, eldest son of Yuki Hideyasu — Ieyasu's second son,
and Lord of Fukui Domain — would prove the greatest contributor on the battlefield.

Matsudaira Tadateru, twenty-three, the sixth son of Ieyasu, was known as the estranged
son — noted for how little he resembled his father.

Tokugawa Yorinobu, only thirteen, stood as the first Lord of Kii Tokugawa — a symbolic
leader of the Tokugawa family, present to represent the bloodline itself.

And Tokugawa Yoshinao, fifteen, first Lord of Owari Tokugawa, was there to gain the
combat experience that would shape him into a ruling lord.

Four sons, four grandsons of the Shogun's line — encircling Osaka Castle from every side.

---

## 5. The Generation of 1600: The Youth in War

Some of the boys who fought at Osaka were barely into their teens.

Tokugawa Yoshinao, fifteen, fought at Domyoji and Komatsuji — sent to gain valuable,
real combat experience for his future as a ruling lord. He survived.

Tokugawa Yorinobu, about thirteen, was positioned near his brother Yoshinao — present
as a symbolic figurehead of Tokugawa power. He, too, survived.

But not every child of this war lived to see its end.

Sanada Daisuke, about thirteen, the eldest son of Sanada Yukimura, stayed inside Osaka
Castle at his father's side — a doomed resistance alongside his clan. On the very day
his father fell, he committed ritual suicide beside Toyotomi Hideyori, ending his short
life.

---

## 6. The Pillars of Resistance

Mori Katsunaga. One of the "Five Elders" of Osaka, who became a ronin after the Battle
of Sekigahara.

He is praised, alongside Sanada Yukimura, as one of the two pillars of the Toyotomi
army. At Tennoji, he launched a fierce frontal assault on the Tokugawa forces — and
achieved major results, specifically credited with the death of the brave Tokugawa
general, Honda Tadamasa.

He fought valiantly to the very end. And then, like so many of his comrades, he chose
to take his own life rather than surrender.

---

## 7. Factions Within the Fortress

Even within Osaka Castle, the Toyotomi were not of one mind.

Ono Harunaga — the moderate. A peacemaker who favored negotiations to save the Toyotomi
lineage, he served as the central coordinator of overall command. When the castle fell,
he committed suicide alongside his lords.

Ono Harufusa — the hardliner. Harunaga's younger brother, and a warmonger who fiercely
advocated for continuing the war. He actively led offensives on the Okayama front,
inflicting heavy damage on the Tokugawa. But when the castle burned, he escaped —
and his ultimate fate remains unknown to history to this day.

Peace and defiance, coordination and chaos — both lived inside the same collapsing
fortress.

---

## 8. The Climax at Tennoji

This was the breaking point.

Sanada Yukimura's charge: a suicidal assault breaking through the Shogunate's lines,
not once, but three separate times.

He drove straight toward Ieyasu's own headquarters — coming within striking distance,
close enough to push the Shogun himself to the very brink of suicide.

Matsudaira Tadanao's forces fiercely fought back against Sanada's charge, and were
eventually credited with killing him.

And at the absolute front line stood Ii Naotaka, only twenty-five, inheritor of the
elite "Red Brigade" — the Akazonae. He held firm, and in doing so, saved the Shogun.

---

## 9. Japan's Greatest Warrior — Sanada Yukimura

Sanada Yukimura, also known as Nobushige. Age forty-nine. The last great hero of the
Sengoku period.

Imprisoned in Kudoyama following the Battle of Sekigahara, he made a dramatic return
for both the Winter and Summer Campaigns at Osaka.

Leading a suicidal charge against Ieyasu's main camp at Tennoji, his ferocity reportedly
drove Ieyasu to the brink of taking his own life — before Yukimura himself finally fell
in battle.

To this day, he is remembered as Japan's greatest warrior.

---

## 10. The Tokugawa Legacy — 260 Years

From Ieyasu, the first Shogun, to Yoshinobu, the fifteenth and last — the Tokugawa
Shogunate lasted two hundred and sixty years.

Its beginning, here at Osaka, marked the arrival of the Edo Period.

And over time, that era progressed toward its own end — the restoration of imperial
rule, through the surrender of political power to the Emperor. The Taisei Hokan.

The Summer Siege of Osaka, 1615. The end of one era — and the beginning of another.
